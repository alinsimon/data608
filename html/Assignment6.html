Hunger in America: The Case for Action

What the data tells us about food insecurity, poverty, and why children who go hungry don’t simply grow out of it

44M Americans are food insecure
1 in 5 School-age children
39.4% Of households below the poverty line
5× More likely without a high school diploma

Food insecurity in America is not random bad luck. It follows poverty. It falls heaviest on children. And those children carry it into adulthood creating a cycle that costs the country more to ignore than to fix.

Each of the five charts below provides evidence for one or more links in this chain. Federal nutrition and education programs are the only proven mechanism to break it.

1Poverty and Hunger Are the Same Problem

State Scatter Poverty Rate vs. Food Insecurity Rate ⓘ Hover any dot for state details • click legend to filter • zoom with the toolbar
Each dot is a state. A state’s poverty rate alone explains 55% of the variation in its food insecurity rate (R² = 0.55). Green dots are outperforming the trend; red dots are worse than their poverty level would predict those states need targeted intervention now.
What we’re asking for
  • Expand the Earned Income Tax Credit it reduces poverty and hunger in a single move
  • Increase dedicated funding for red-zone states facing structural barriers beyond income alone
  • Require food insecurity data to be reported alongside poverty statistics in annual federal filings

2Below the Poverty Line, 4 in 10 Households Go Hungry

Grouped Bar Food Insecurity by Income-to-Poverty Ratio ⓘ Hover for exact rates • click legend to show/hide series
Food insecurity is 39.4% below the poverty line and drops to 7.9% above the 185% SNAP threshold. The dark bars show very low food security 17.6% of the poorest households are regularly skipping meals. That is starvation risk, not inconvenience.
What we’re asking for
  • Protect SNAP at current eligibility thresholds cutting benefits would push millions from 7.9% back toward 39%
  • Expand WIC outreach to families between 130% and 185% of poverty who currently fall through the gap
  • Oppose work requirement changes that remove benefits from families who remain food insecure

3Nearly 1 in 5 Children in a Classroom Right Now Is Hungry

Grouped Bar Food Insecurity Rate by Age Group and Sex ⓘ Hover for exact rates • click legend to filter by sex
School-age children (5–17) face the highest food insecurity of any age group: ~19.5%. Boys and girls are equally affected gender is not a factor here, household income is. Rates fall sharply for seniors because Social Security and Medicare provide the income stability that working-age families with children don’t have.
What we’re asking for
  • Fully fund the National School Lunch and School Breakfast Programs the most direct intervention available
  • Expand summer EBT so children don’t lose meal access when school is out
  • Increase WIC funding to cover all eligible families with children under 5

4Hungry Children Become Hungry Adults. The Cycle Doesn’t Break Itself.

State Scatter Child Food Insecurity Rate vs. Young Adult Rate ⓘ Hover any dot for state name and exact rates • zoom with the toolbar
States where children go hungry are the same states where young adults go hungry (R² = 0.28). Most dots fall below the equal-rates line young adult rates are somewhat lower but in Oklahoma, Arkansas, and Louisiana, more than 1 in 4 young adults remains food insecure. This is structural, not a phase children age out of.
What we’re asking for
  • Prioritize federal food program funding toward states in the upper-right cluster they are pre-loading the next decade of adult poverty
  • Support the Child Tax Credit as a hunger-reduction tool alongside nutrition programs
  • Fund tracking that links childhood food insecurity records to adult workforce and health outcomes

5Education Is the Exit Ramp If We Fund It

Grouped Bar Food Insecurity Rate by Highest Education Level ⓘ Hover for exact rates • click legend to show/hide series
No high school diploma: 30.4% food insecure. College degree: 6.1% food insecure. That is a 5× gap driven by one variable. A hungry child is less likely to graduate. A household without a diploma is five times more likely to go hungry in adulthood. That is the mechanism of the cycle and the reason education investment is hunger prevention.
What we’re asking for
  • Expand Head Start and early childhood nutrition programs they keep children school-ready and reduce the dropout pipeline
  • Fund free community college and vocational credentials for low-income adults without a diploma
  • Protect Pell Grants the primary access route to higher education for first-generation, food-insecure families

The Bottom Line

Five angles, one story: food insecurity is concentrated, predictable, and self-reinforcing. The programs that interrupt this cycle are not expensive measured against what the cycle costs:

  • SNAP proven to reduce food insecurity and improve child development outcomes
  • WIC one of the most cost-effective interventions in the federal budget
  • School Lunch & Summer EBT direct impact on the 1-in-5 figure in Chart 3
  • Head Start & early education the mechanism that breaks the cycle shown in Charts 4 and 5

The data here comes from the same Census Bureau that counts the seats in every Congressional district. Fund these programs or explain to your constituents why 44 million of them don’t deserve a meal.

Data & Methodology

Primary: Census CPS Food Security Supplement, December 2023 (126,832 records, weighted with HHSUPWGT; food insecurity = HRFS12M1 ∈ {2,3}). Age-group rates are weighted prevalence by state × sex × age group. Additional: USDA ERS Food Security Reports 2024 (state and demographic tables, 3-year averages); Census ACS 1-year 2022, table B17001 (state poverty rates). All charts built in Python with Plotly. Limitation: Chart 4 is cross-sectional, not a longitudinal panel it compares children and young adults in the same state and year, not the same individuals over time.
