Do stricter firearm control laws help reduce firearm mortality?

Data Sources: CDC Open Data API, Giffords Law Center Annual Scorecard (2024 grades) | Date: March 2026 | Alinzon Simon

Research Question

Gun legislation varies substantially across American states, ranging from minimal regulations to comprehensive regulatory frameworks including background checks, waiting periods, and licensing requirements. This analysis examines whether these policy differences correlate with measurable differences in firearm mortality outcomes.

Using mortality data from the CDC Open Data API (Mapping Injury, Overdose, and Violence dataset) and gun law grades from the Giffords Law Center Annual Gun Law Scorecard, we quantified the relationship between regulatory strictness and death rates across all states.

Primary Finding

r = -0.735

Our analysis reveals a strong negative correlation between gun law strictness and firearm mortality rates. States with stricter regulations consistently show lower death rates:

  • Very strict states (A grade): 8.5 deaths per 100,000
  • Very lax states (F grade): 20.8 deaths per 100,000
  • Difference: 2.5 fold variation in mortality rates

Interpretation

Each point represents one state plotted by law strictness (x-axis, F to A grade) and firearm mortality rate (y-axis, deaths per 100,000). The regression line demonstrates the negative relationship between these variables.

The pattern is consistent: states with weaker laws (Mississippi, Louisiana, Wyoming) cluster in the upper-left quadrant with higher death rates. States with stronger laws (Massachusetts, New York, California) cluster in the lower-right with substantially lower rates. This distribution suggests a systematic relationship rather than random variation.

A scatter plot effectively displays bivariate relationships while preserving individual data points. This maintains analytical transparency and allows viewers to identify specific states or patterns. The regression line quantifies the overall trend.

Visualization 2: Every State's Story

Paneled Horizontal Bar Chart

Interpretation

These charts present all states as horizontal bars organized into five separate panels , one for each gun law grade tier (F through A). The x-axis shows firearm deaths per 100,000 people on a shared scale (0-30) across all panels, enabling direct cross category comparison while keeping each tier readable on its own. All jurisdictions within each panel are sorted by death rate.

The Very Lax (F) panel is dominated by long red bars, with states like Mississippi (27.6), New Mexico (26.6), and Alaska (24.8) exceeding 20 deaths per 100,000. Moving down through the panels, bar lengths shrink progressively and colors shift from red to yellow. The Very Strict (A) panel shows predominantly short yellow bars, with Hawaii (3.9), Massachusetts (4.1), New Jersey (4.2), and New York (4.6) all under 5 per 100,000 with the notable exception of the District of Columbia (21.8), which reflects the unique challenges of an urban jurisdiction surrounded by states with weaker laws.

Splitting states into grade specific panels reduces visual clutter while maintaining access to individual state values. The shared x-axis preserves proportional comparison across all panels, and the within panel ordering by death rate allows ranking within each policy tier.

Visualization 3: The Big Picture

Horizontal Bar Chart

Interpretation

This chart aggregates state level data into five categories based on gun law grades, displaying the mean mortality rate for each category. This reduces dimensionality from state level data to 5 summary statistics.

The progression is monotonic and substantial. Each incremental increase in law strictness corresponds to a measurable decrease in average mortality rate:

  • Very Lax to Very Strict: 59% reduction in average death rate
  • No overlap between extreme categories indicates robust effect size
Aggregated bar charts sacrifice granularity for clarity. This reduction simplifies pattern recognition and is appropriate when the high level trend is the primary message.

Data Sources

  • CDC Open Data API: Firearm mortality rates per 100,000 people from the "Mapping Injury, Overdose, and Violence-State" dataset
  • Giffords Law Center Annual Gun Law Scorecard: 2024 state gun law letter grades (A through F) converted to 5 point ordinal scale for statistical analysis

Analytical Approach

Gun law letter grades (A through F) were converted to a 5 point ordinal scale (F=1 to A=5) to enable correlation analysis.

Limitations and Interpretation

A statistically significant negative correlation (r = -0.735, p < 0.001) exists between gun law strictness and firearm mortality rates across state level observations.

Direct causation. Multiple confounding variables influence firearm mortality including socioeconomic factors, cultural contexts, population density, and enforcement effectiveness. The observed correlation, while strong, represents association rather than definitive causal proof.
