Has the Fed Fulfilled Its Dual Mandate?

25 Years of Data (2002-2026) | Assignment 2 | Alinzon Simon

The Question

Congress gave the Federal Reserve two jobs: keep inflation low and keep unemployment low. The catch? These goals often conflict. Lowering rates to fight unemployment can spike inflation. Raising rates to cool inflation can kill jobs.

We're analyzing 25 years of data to see how well the Fed balanced this tightrope act.

Story Development Focus: This analysis uses a data driven narrative framework with Plotly interactive visualizations. The story follows a chronological progression - showing the dual mandate challenge, the Fed's responses, major crisis moments, and the fundamental economic tradeoff. Each visualization builds on the previous one to answer our central question.

1. The Dual Mandate Over Time

What you're seeing: Two timeseries showing inflation (top) and unemployment (bottom) from 2002-2026.

Why it matters: When these move in opposite directions, the Fed has to pick which problem to solve first.

Key takeaway: The Fed kept inflation near 2% for most of this period, except during the 2008 crisis (deflation) and 2021-2023 (9% spike).

2. Fed's Response: Did Their Actions Make Sense?

What you're seeing: The thick green line is the Fed Funds Rate (what they control). The other lines show what was happening with inflation and unemployment.

Why it matters: This shows whether the Fed's decisions were logical given the economic conditions.

Key takeaway: The Fed responded fast in crises (2008, COVID) by cutting rates to near-zero. When inflation hit 9% in 2022, they raised rates from 0% to 5.3% in under two years.

3. The Big Tests: Three Major Crises

What you're seeing: Shaded regions highlight three crisis periods where the Fed was really tested.

Why it matters: Anyone can manage during calm times. Crises reveal how well the Fed actually performs.

Key takeaway: 2008 Financial Crisis - cut rates to zero. COVID-19 - emergency cuts within days. 2022 Inflation - aggressive hikes to regain control. All three show the Fed willing to act decisively.

4. The Fundamental Question: Can You Have Both?

What you're seeing: Each dot is a month, plotted by unemployment (x-axis) and inflation (y-axis). Color = year.

Why it matters: If low inflation and low unemployment can coexist, dots cluster in bottom-left. If there's always a tradeoff, they'd form a diagonal line.

Key takeaway: The correlation is moderate (-0.42), not a strong tradeoff. The 2015-2019 period averaged 4.4% unemployment and 1.6% inflation - near perfect execution. COVID created the outlier (14.8% unemployment, 0.3% inflation). The 2022 spike hit 9% inflation with only 3.6% unemployment. Recent data (2023-2026) shows inflation falling from 9% to 2.4% while unemployment stayed around 4% - a successful soft landing.

Bottom Line

Has the Fed fulfilled its dual mandate? Mostly yes.

For the majority of 25 years, they kept inflation close to 2% and unemployment trending down. Their crisis responses (2008, COVID) were swift and prevented worse outcomes. The 2015-2019 period was nearly perfect execution.

The 2021-2023 inflation spike is the big black mark. Whether they kept rates too low for too long is debatable, but hitting 9% inflation (the worst in 40 years) shows they were late to act. That said, the current soft landing (bringing inflation back down to 2.4% without causing a recession) is impressive.
